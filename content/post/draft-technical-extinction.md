We love to divide ourselves into cultures and groups. We love to place things in boxes (both real and imagined) and to categorize them. These camps provide us with a measure of safety where certain elements and conditions are a given. Belonging to a culture can give us a sense of identity, e.g. "I'm a doctor", "I'm a programer", or "I'm a Democrat", etc. Historicaly, these cultures helped us survive. But if we're not careful, these same cultures can kill us.

How often do you ascribe your identity to one of the following camps:

* Male vs. female
* Science vs. religion
* Democrat vs. Republican
* America vs. everyone else
* UCLA vs. USC
* C# vs. Java
* Windows vs. Linux
* iPhone vs. Android
* Chrome vs FireFox vs. IE

Interestingly, whenever someone comes into our group, we trumpt and celebrate these new converts and welcome them with open arms parading their newfound freedom from whatever oppression was formerly theirs. Meanwhile, when someone leaves our camp, we rail upon them and call them traitors to the cause. We shun and abandon our former friends and riddicule them mercilessly.

These groups originated based upon a specific need, whether physical, emotional, social, spiritual, or otherwise--all of which can typically be found on [Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maslow's_hierarchy_of_needs). Because of this, any questioning, compliants, or other introspection into the group is often viewed as a direct assault on the core tenets of that group and will thus be vehemently opposed. Many times these disagreements arise internally, which results in splinter groups that break away from the parent to form their own faction. This is also why those who leave are branded apostates and labeled traitors to the original cause.

"Group think" can be awesome--especially when people of divergent views, backgrounds, experiences, and opinions can come together for a common good and can create together a shared vision working toward a mutual goal. But "group think" can be incredibly dangerious when we live in an echo chamber because it doesn't bring any new understanding or insight to the table. Instead, "group think" often lets us pat ourselves on the back telling us how great we are.

Our "rightness" leaves us totally blind. How dare someone question me! This is a product of Level 3 culture--our culture is better than your culture. I am better than you. I might be right, but with that kind of thinking, I won't be around much longer either.

In evolutionary terms, homogeneity--the act of being same--always results in one thing: extinction. The only protection against extinction was hetrogenious evolution. The act of becoming more **diverse** protected a species from complete and utter annihilation.

As a member of any given group or division, we have our closely and strongly held views. Yet more often than not, progress in the form major breakthroughs  comes as a result of a **break with** existing views. For example, modern germ theory, the flat- vs. round-earth model, the sun being the center of our solar system, and Newtonian and Einsteinian physics.

In the purely technical terms, extinction would be synonmous with obsolecense. Who doubts that strongly coupling yourself today to Netscape 3.0 is a good idea? What about coding our new system exclusively for the IBM PC jr? We should definitely design systems that target the new Intel 286 microprocessor. Granted, these are contrived examples are silly, but they should give us pause. They should cause us to question our own virtue. We should be asking: "In what way is my culture X like obsolete technology Y?"

- Variety of solutions
- Learn from others
- Everything we have today was someone questioning the status quo
- Look outside yourself
- it's harder, but worth it
- willing to receive truth vs unwilling and dogmatic

Mono-culture for running software often masks bugs. How often have you found bugs in your own code by running things in a slightly different environment.
