<p align="justify">When working on a new domain model a few days ago, we ran into an interesting issue.&# 160; What happens when an aggregate has shared data?&# 160; You know, data that is common or shared between aggregate roots?&# 160; This is a <a href="http://tech.groups.yahoo.com/group/domaindrivendesign/message/17145">question that also recently</a> came up on the Domain Driven Design Yahoo Group, we had generally the same question but independent of the Yahoo Group.</p> <p align="justify">For purposes of example, we will use the canonical sales/order model.&# 160; Let’s suppose that there is a business rule which states that unconfirmed accounts may only place a maximum of two orders which combined total no more than $100 in order to limit fraud.&# 160; It’s a simple enough business requirement, right?&# 160; But there is a small challenge, this particular business requirement seems to span multiple aggregate roots which each contain additional business rules surrounding the acceptance of an order.</p> <p align="justify">So what do you do?&# 160; First of all, from a technical perspective, it can be solved several different ways.&# 160; But the reason that we’re working with a domain model is because we want the benefits of a domain model.&# 160; In order to get those benefits we have to follow the rules of a domain model.</p> <p align="justify">The discussion on the Yahoo Group surrounded that of using double-dispatch techniques in order to retrieve the data needed by the aggregate root.&# 160; Ever since attending Udi’s SOA course in Austin last December, I generally dislike using double-dispatch techniques within the domain model.</p> <p align="justify">Another simple technique is to have a shared entity object between the aggregates.&# 160; While it works and NHibernate will let you do this, it’s generally a bad idea because an aggregate is a unit of consistency.&# 160; It is atomic—all or nothing.&# 160; Having a shared entity violates that atomicity and autonomy of an aggregate.&# 160; While technically speaking we can do this, we probably shouldn’t as it violates one of the fundamental principles of DDD.</p> <p align="justify">It may sound silly but at this point we were a little bit stuck.&# 160; When using event sourcing to rebuild your Order aggregate, it has no concept of the combined order total of all orders for a customer.&# 160; It only cares about itself and not other orders.&# 160; So how do we enforce the business requirement?&# 160; We don’t like the idea of double dispatch and we don’t want to have some kind of shared entity.&# 160; What can we do?</p> <p align="justify">The answer is to introduce another aggregate—an OrderTotals aggregate.&# 160; When the PlaceOrderCommand is dispatched to system, it is handled by two aggregates within the service layer.&# 160; The first aggregate, OrderTotals, ensures that the command can be processed.&# 160; If not, it returns a fault indicating why.&# 160; If we receive a fault, we stop processing.&# 160; Otherwise, the Order aggregate receives the command and processes it.&# 160; When finished, it publishes the OrderAccepted event (to which the OrderTotals aggregate subscribes) and the transaction commits.</p> <p align="justify">All in all, it’s fairly simple.&# 160; This idea applies beyond situations where event sourcing is being used to rebuild the domain entities by replaying the events.&# 160; It addresses the fundamental issue of how you deal with rules that span multiple aggregate roots.</p>
