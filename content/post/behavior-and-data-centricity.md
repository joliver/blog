<p>As I look back at applications and systems that I have been involved with over the years, what strikes me is how data-centric those applications were.&# 160; In fact, the driving force behind most of those applications was the data.&# 160; The behavior of an application was looked at as merely if statements and for loops.&# 160; It was inconsequential.&# 160; The other thing that strikes me about all of these applications is that, because they were all about the data, they were incredibly dumb.&# 160; Literally.&# 160; The applications didn’t “think” for themselves.&# 160; The “logic” governing new data was a purely technical consequence littered with database queries and web service calls, etc.</p> <p>In the real world, this would be similar to viewing a human as an inanimate object—like a rock.&# 160; In a data-centric mindset, it’s all about the properties of the thing you’re dealing with, e.g., the name, size, weight, color, shape, etc.&# 160; In the case of the rock and human, you get the data you want and continue.&# 160; This is why a data-centric view is flawed when complexity merits a behavioral view.</p> <p>In the case of the rock, it just sits there and doesn’t do anything, just like the dumb, data container that it is.&# 160; You do everything to it.&# 160; But when you interact with a person in real life, the person can understand and work out problems on their own in a behavioral fashion.&# 160; You can’t see their thought processes, you simply feed in sensory information (messages to be consumed through the senses) and the person behaves and interacts with his/her environment (publishes messages).&# 160; The person has no getters/setters.&# 160; You simply provide a command and the person decides if he/she wants to respond.&# 160; (As an aside, I think we should refer to all behavior objects as females.)</p> <p>How does this apply in DDDD?&# 160; Simple.&# 160; You give an object new information to consider.&# 160; It internalizes the information and when it receives a “command” (a request to perform some action), it decides how it wants to respond based upon the sum total of the object’s experience during its lifetime.&# 160; In other words, all of the data that you have ever provided to the object constitutes the object’s experience and understanding and it behaves and makes decisions (publishes a message) based upon that collective experience.</p> <p>In a nutshell, that’s the power behind behavioral programming.&# 160; It’s a very <em>Tell, Don’t Ask</em> style in which your objects get the job done, rather than you doing everything.&# 160; And if you don’t like what an particular object is doing?&# 160; Send it out to pasture and put something else in its place.</p>
